Arctic Wildlife

Polar Bear

Ursus maritimus

Distribution

Arctic Circle: Canada, Alaska, Greenland, Norway, Russia

Habitat

Sea ice and coastal areas

Size

Males: 350-700kg (770-1,540lbs), Females: 150-300kg (330-660lbs)

Taxonomy

Family: Ursidae, Order: Carnivora

The polar bear is the world's largest land carnivore and a powerful symbol of the Arctic. These magnificent predators are superbly adapted to life in the harsh Arctic environment, with thick insulating fur, a layer of blubber up to 10cm thick, and large paws that act as snowshoes. Their white fur provides camouflage against the snow and ice, while their black skin helps absorb heat from the sun.

Polar bears can smell a seal's breathing hole in the ice from more than 1km (0.6 miles) away, and can detect a seal on the ice from nearly 30km (18 miles) away!

Polar bears are excellent swimmers, capable of swimming for days at a time across open water. They primarily hunt seals, waiting patiently by breathing holes or breaking through seal dens in the snow. Climate change is dramatically affecting their hunting patterns as Arctic sea ice melts earlier each spring and forms later each fall.

Conservation Status: Vulnerable (IUCN Red List)

Population decreasing - estimated 22,000-31,000 remaining worldwide

Major threats to polar bears include:

  • Climate change and loss of sea ice habitat
  • Pollution accumulating in Arctic food chains
  • Human-wildlife conflicts
  • Oil and gas development in the Arctic
  • Historical overhunting (now regulated)

Conservation efforts focus on protecting critical habitat, reducing greenhouse gas emissions, and minimizing human-bear conflicts. Polar bears are protected under international agreements and by the governments of all five range states (Canada, Denmark/Greenland, Norway, Russia, and the United States). Ecotourism programs help support local communities while raising awareness about polar bear conservation.
