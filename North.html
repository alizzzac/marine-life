Marine Mammals

Blue Whale

Balaenoptera musculus

Distribution

All oceans except the Arctic

Habitat

Open ocean, often near continental shelves

Size

25-30m (82-98ft) length, 100-150 tons weight

Taxonomy

Family: Balaenopteridae, Order: Cetacea

Population

10,000-25,000 (pre-whaling: ~350,000)

The blue whale is the largest animal ever known to have existed, dwarfing even the biggest dinosaurs. Their heart alone can weigh as much as a small car (400-600kg), and their tongue weighs as much as an elephant. A single adult blue whale can consume up to 4 tons of krill per day during feeding season.

A blue whale's spout can reach up to 9m (30ft) in height, and their low-frequency vocalizations can travel hundreds of miles underwater—the loudest sound made by any animal. Click for more facts.

Blue whales are so large that some of their blood vessels are big enough for a small child to swim through. Their aorta (main artery) has a diameter of about 23cm (9 inches)!

Conservation Status: Endangered (IUCN Red List)

Population recovering but still at 3-11% of pre-whaling numbers

Blue whales are baleen whales, meaning they filter-feed using keratin plates instead of teeth. They primarily eat tiny shrimp-like creatures called krill, consuming up to 40 million krill per day. Their massive size is possible because the buoyancy of water supports their weight, unlike land animals.

Major threats to blue whales include:

  • Historical commercial whaling (nearly drove them to extinction)
  • Ship strikes (collisions with large vessels)
  • Ocean noise pollution (disrupts communication)
  • Climate change (affects krill populations)
  • Entanglement in fishing gear

Blue whale calves are the biggest babies on Earth, born at about 7m (23ft) long and weighing 2-3 tons. They gain about 90kg (200lbs) per day from their mother's rich milk. Click for more.

A blue whale mother produces approximately 200-400 liters of milk per day with a fat content of 35-50% (human milk has about 4% fat content). This incredibly rich milk helps the calf grow rapidly.

Conservation successes include:

  • International protection since 1966 (International Whaling Commission)
  • Population increases in some areas (e.g., California coast)
  • Ship speed reduction zones in critical habitats
  • Improved monitoring and research

The blue whale's recovery demonstrates nature's resilience when given protection. Their continued conservation helps maintain the health of ocean ecosystems worldwide.
