Arctic Marine Mammals

Narwhal

Monodon monoceros

Distribution

Arctic waters around Canada, Greenland, Norway, and Russia

Habitat

Deep fjords and offshore pack ice

Size

4-5m (13-16ft) including tusk, 800-1,600kg (1,800-3,500lbs)

Taxonomy

Family: Monodontidae (with beluga whales), Order: Cetacea

The narwhal, often called the "unicorn of the sea," is a medium-sized Arctic whale known for the spectacular spiral tusk that grows through the upper lip of males. This tusk is actually an elongated left canine tooth that can grow up to 3m (10ft) long. Narwhals are deep-diving predators that feed on fish, squid, and shrimp in the icy waters of the Arctic.

The narwhal's tusk is one of the most sensitive teeth known to science, containing up to 10 million nerve endings that may help detect changes in water temperature, pressure, and salinity!

Narwhals are highly social animals that travel in groups called pods, typically consisting of 15-20 individuals though sometimes aggregating in the hundreds. They communicate with a variety of clicks, whistles, and knocks. These Arctic specialists are uniquely adapted to life under sea ice, capable of diving deeper than 1,500m (4,900ft) and holding their breath for up to 25 minutes.

Conservation Status: Near Threatened (IUCN Red List)

Estimated population of 123,000 mature individuals (2017 assessment)

Major threats to narwhals include:

  • Climate change reducing Arctic sea ice
  • Increased shipping and industrial activity
  • Hunting by indigenous communities
  • Ocean noise pollution disrupting communication
  • Entanglement in fishing gear

Conservation efforts focus on sustainable hunting quotas, protecting critical habitat, and minimizing human disturbances. Narwhals hold significant cultural importance for Inuit communities, who have hunted them sustainably for thousands of years. Research using satellite tags is helping scientists better understand narwhal migration patterns and habitat needs in our rapidly changing Arctic.
