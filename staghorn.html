Coral Reef Ecosystems

Staghorn Coral

Acropora cervicornis

Distribution

Western Atlantic, Caribbean, Bahamas, Florida

Environment

Shallow reef environments (1-30m depth)

Growth Rate

5-10 cm per year (one of fastest growing corals)

Taxonomy

Family: Acroporidae, Order: Scleractinia (stony corals)

Staghorn coral (Acropora cervicornis) is a branching, stony coral with cylindrical branches that resemble deer antlers. These corals are among the most important reef-building species in the Caribbean, providing essential habitat for numerous marine organisms. Their complex structure offers shelter for fish, crustaceans, and other reef creatures.

A single staghorn coral colony can grow over 2 meters (6.5 feet) in diameter and live for hundreds of years, with some colonies estimated to be over 500 years old!

Staghorn corals reproduce both sexually through mass spawning events (typically after full moons in late summer) and asexually through fragmentation. This ability to regrow from broken pieces makes them particularly resilient to physical disturbances like storms when water conditions are healthy.

Conservation Status: Critically Endangered (IUCN Red List)

Staghorn coral populations have declined by 80-98% across their range since 1980 due to disease, climate change, and other stressors.

Primary threats include:

  • White band disease
  • Ocean acidification
  • Bleaching from warming waters
  • Physical damage from storms and human activity
  • Poor water quality from coastal development

Conservation efforts include coral nurseries where fragments are grown and later transplanted to restore damaged reefs. Many organizations also work to protect remaining populations through marine protected areas and water quality improvements. Citizen scientists can help by participating in reef monitoring programs.
