Marine Mammal Conservation

Southern Sea Otter

Enhydra lutris nereis

Distribution

Central California coastline

Habitat

Kelp forests and rocky shores

Size

1.2-1.5m (4-5ft) length, 20-30kg (45-65lbs)

Taxonomy

Family: Mustelidae (weasels), Subfamily: Lutrinae

Population

~3,000 individuals (2023 estimate)

The southern sea otter is a charismatic marine mammal and a keystone species of California's coastal ecosystems. Unlike other marine mammals, sea otters lack blubber and instead rely on their incredibly dense fur (up to 1 million hairs per square inch!) to stay warm in chilly Pacific waters.

Sea otters are one of the few mammals besides primates that use tools. They often use rocks to crack open shellfish while floating on their backs!

Conservation Status: Threatened (U.S. ESA)

While populations have rebounded from near extinction, they remain at about 30% of historical levels.

Sea otters play a crucial role in maintaining healthy kelp forests by controlling sea urchin populations. Without otters, urchins can overgraze kelp, creating "urchin barrens" that support far less marine life.

Major threats to southern sea otters include:

  • Oil spills (their fur loses insulation when oiled)
  • Shark bites (increasing in frequency)
  • Infectious diseases from coastal pollution
  • Food limitation in parts of their range
  • Historical overhunting for their luxurious fur

A sea otter's daily food intake is about 25% of its body weight - equivalent to a 150lb human eating 37lbs of food each day!

Conservation successes include:

  • International protection from hunting (1911 Fur Seal Treaty)
  • Successful reintroduction programs
  • Oil spill response programs
  • Habitat protection measures
  • Public education campaigns

The southern sea otter's recovery demonstrates how protecting a single keystone species can benefit entire ecosystems. Their continued protection helps maintain biodiversity in California's coastal waters.
