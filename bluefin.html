Pelagic Fish Species

Atlantic Bluefin Tuna

Thunnus thynnus

Distribution

North Atlantic Ocean and Mediterranean Sea

Environment

Open ocean, surface to 1000m depth

Size

Up to 3m (10ft) and 680kg (1,500lbs)

Taxonomy

Family: Scombridae, Order: Scombriformes

The Atlantic bluefin tuna is one of the largest, fastest, and most magnificent fish in the ocean. These powerful swimmers can reach speeds of 70 km/h (43 mph) and are known for their incredible migrations across entire ocean basins. Their streamlined bodies and specialized physiology allow them to maintain body temperatures higher than the surrounding water, making them highly efficient predators.

Bluefin tuna can cross the Atlantic Ocean in less than 60 days, and some individuals have been tracked swimming from North America to Europe and back in a single year! Click for more facts.

Bluefin tuna are warm-blooded, which is rare among fish. This adaptation allows them to thrive in colder waters and swim at incredible speeds. Their bodies can be 20°F (11°C) warmer than the surrounding water, giving them a significant advantage when hunting.

Bluefin tuna are apex predators that play a crucial role in marine ecosystems. Their diet consists mainly of smaller fish like herring, mackerel, and squid. They hunt by sight and often work together to herd schools of fish into tight balls before attacking.

Bluefin Tuna Population Status

Select a region to see tuna population trends:

Conservation Status: Endangered (IUCN Red List)

Western Atlantic populations have declined by over 80% since 1970 due to overfishing.

Major threats to bluefin tuna include:

  • Overfishing for the lucrative sushi/sashimi market
  • Bycatch in other fisheries
  • Habitat degradation
  • Climate change affecting prey distribution

Conservation efforts include strict international fishing quotas, improved monitoring systems, and aquaculture research. The species is managed by the International Commission for the Conservation of Atlantic Tunas (ICCAT). Sustainable seafood choices and supporting responsible fishing practices can help protect these magnificent fish.
