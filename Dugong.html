Marine Mammals

Dugong

Dugong dugon

Distribution

Coastal waters of Indo-Pacific region

Habitat

Shallow, protected bays and mangrove channels

Size

Up to 3m (10ft) and 400kg (900lbs)

Taxonomy

Order: Sirenia, Family: Dugongidae

The dugong, sometimes called the "sea cow," is a gentle marine mammal that grazes on seagrass meadows in warm coastal waters. As the only strictly marine herbivorous mammal, dugongs play a vital role in maintaining healthy seagrass ecosystems. Their slow, graceful movements and rounded bodies give them a distinctive appearance unlike any other marine creature.

Dugongs can live for 70 years or more, and mothers nurse their calves for up to 18 months - one of the longest nursing periods of any marine mammal! Click for more facts.

Dugongs are thought to have inspired ancient tales of mermaids with their human-like eyes and tendency to sit upright in the water while nursing their young. They can hold their breath for up to six minutes before surfacing to breathe.

Dugongs are closely related to manatees but differ in several ways, including their dolphin-like tail flukes and specialized snouts for uprooting seagrass. An adult dugong can eat up to 40kg of seagrass daily, carefully cropping the plants to encourage healthy regrowth. This grazing behavior helps maintain the biodiversity of seagrass meadows.

Conservation Status: Vulnerable (IUCN Red List)

Many populations have declined by more than 30% over the last 60 years.

Major threats to dugongs include:

  • Habitat loss from coastal development
  • Seagrass destruction from pollution and dredging
  • Entanglement in fishing nets
  • Boat strikes
  • Historical hunting for meat and oil

Conservation efforts focus on protecting seagrass habitats, establishing marine protected areas, and working with local communities. In some cultures, dugongs hold significant cultural importance and feature in traditional stories and art. Ecotourism programs are helping raise awareness while providing alternative livelihoods to hunting.
