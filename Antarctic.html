Southern Ocean Wildlife

Antarctic Krill

Euphausia superba

Distribution

Circumpolar in Southern Ocean

Habitat

Open ocean (pelagic), sea ice edge

Size

6 cm (2.4 in) average length, 2 grams weight

Taxonomy

Order: Euphausiacea, Family: Euphausiidae

Unique Feature

Bioluminescent photophores

Antarctic krill are the keystone species of the Southern Ocean ecosystem, forming the critical link between phytoplankton and higher predators. These small crustaceans exist in astonishing numbers, with a total biomass estimated at 379 million tons - one of the most abundant animal species on Earth. Their massive swarms can stretch for kilometers and are visible from space.

A single cubic meter of ocean water can contain up to 30,000 individual krill! Their swarms are so dense they can turn the water pinkish-red.

Krill have evolved remarkable adaptations for survival in frigid Antarctic waters:

  • Antifreeze proteins that prevent ice crystal formation
  • Daily vertical migrations (up to 1,000m depth)
  • Ability to survive months without food in winter
  • Specialized filtering appendages for efficient phytoplankton feeding
  • Bioluminescent organs for communication

Conservation Status: Not Evaluated (IUCN)

While abundant, krill face growing threats from climate change and commercial fishing

Krill are the foundation of the Antarctic food web, supporting:

  • Whales (a blue whale eats up to 4 tons daily!)
  • Seals (especially crabeater seals)
  • Penguins and other seabirds
  • Squid and fish species

Major threats to krill populations include:

  • Climate change reducing sea ice habitat
  • Ocean acidification affecting reproduction
  • Commercial harvesting for aquaculture feed and omega-3 supplements
  • Changes in phytoplankton communities

The Commission for the Conservation of Antarctic Marine Living Resources (CCAMLR) manages krill fisheries with precautionary catch limits. However, scientists emphasize the need for marine protected areas and continued monitoring as the Antarctic environment changes rapidly.
