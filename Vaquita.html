Critically Endangered Species

Vaquita

Phocoena sinus

Distribution

Northern Gulf of California, Mexico

Habitat

Shallow, murky lagoons near coastline

Size

1.5m (5ft) length, 55kg (120lbs) weight

Taxonomy

Family: Phocoenidae (porpoises)

Population

Fewer than 10 individuals remain (2023 estimate)

The vaquita is the world's most endangered marine mammal, with possibly fewer than 10 individuals remaining in the wild. This small porpoise was only discovered in 1958 and is endemic to Mexico's Gulf of California. Vaquitas are easily recognized by their distinctive dark eye rings and lip patches, giving them a "panda-like" appearance.

Vaquita means "little cow" in Spanish. They are the smallest cetaceans in the world and have the most limited range of any marine mammal.

CRITICAL STATUS: IMMINENT EXTINCTION

The vaquita population has declined by 98% since 2011 due to bycatch in illegal gillnets. Without immediate, drastic action, this species will go extinct within years.

Vaquitas face one primary threat:

  • Illegal gillnet fishing for totoaba fish (also endangered)
The totoaba's swim bladder is prized in Chinese traditional medicine, fetching up to $46,000/kg on the black market. Vaquitas become entangled in these nets and drown.

Conservation Efforts

Despite the dire situation, there is still hope:

  • Gillnet ban in vaquita habitat (2017)
  • Increased enforcement by Mexican Navy
  • International conservation partnerships
  • Attempts at captive breeding (unsuccessful)
  • Development of alternative fishing gear

In 2021, Mexico launched the "Zero Tolerance" initiative to combat illegal fishing in the Vaquita Refuge Area.

The vaquita's story represents the complex intersection of wildlife conservation, illegal trade, and local livelihoods. While the situation appears bleak, conservationists emphasize that vaquitas are resilient animals that can recover if given protection from gillnets. Their survival depends on eliminating illegal fishing in their remaining habitat.
